The Indoor Frontier Introduction Dyllan Haggard · 2026

Indoor soccer has existed in the United States for four decades. For most of that time, nobody has written a data paper about it.

That is the remarkable, quiet fact this paper begins with. Baseball has Moneyball. Basketball has Dean Oliver. Hockey has Corsi and Fenwick. The MASL, America's premier professional indoor soccer league, has box scores, YouTube streams, and a small but fierce community of people who have built the sport from the ground up generation after generation.

However, the sport has no public analytics literature. No expected-goals model tuned to a compressed field with dasherboards. No formal taxonomy of tactical styles. No published study of what actually predicts winning at the MASL level. Google the phrase "MASL analytics" and you'll get fewer results than a search for almost any college club sport.

That gap is not a reflection of the sport's quality. It is a reflection of a sport that has been moving faster than anyone has had time to measure. Indoor soccer averages between eight and ten goals per game on a field roughly one-fifth the size of a traditional pitch, with unlimited on-the-fly substitutions and a dasherboard wall that functions as an eleventh tactical element. It is faster than outdoor soccer, denser than basketball, and more chaotic than hockey. And until recently, nobody had been hired to write the numbers down.

What This Paper Is

This is the first part of a longer series. The mission is simple: take the tools that modernized every other professional sport and apply them, seriously and from first principles, to the MASL. Not as a transplant of outdoor-soccer metrics, but as a fresh analytical frame built for the game as it actually is.

The research pairs league-wide trends with two years of granular event-tagging, shot-charting, and opposition analysis built inside the St. Louis Ambush by the paper's author, a team that has gone from seventeen points to forty-two in back-to-back seasons and reached the 2026 semifinals. The analytics function was built, from scratch, in the same window. That case study is not an aside. It is a proof of concept for what the supporting layer around a serious coaching staff can look like.

Why Now
The sport is starting to catch up with itself.
A handful of MASL organizations have started integrating analysts into their operations, the St. Louis Ambush and Kansas City Comets among them. The league itself now has a dedicated staff writer pushing data-driven storytelling out to a growing audience. The pieces are in motion. Someone was going to write the analytical literature eventually, and this paper is part of that.
There is no single way to win indoor soccer. There are several, and each one leaves its own fingerprint in the data. — A working thesis

01
The Game Is Different

What the numbers say about indoor soccer.

Apply outdoor-soccer metrics to an indoor game and almost every signal becomes noise. The first job is the simplest: redefine the measurement layer from the floor up.

Every sport that modernized its analytics went through the same opening move. Redefining what a possession, a shot, or a turnover actually meant inside its own rules.

Basketball did it when Dean Oliver separated true shooting percentage from field-goal percentage. Hockey did it when Corsi counted shot attempts instead of just goals. Outdoor soccer did it when Opta introduced expected goals and reframed what "a chance" really was. Each of those leaps had the same architecture: reject the inherited metric, rebuild from the ground up, and let the data map the sport as it actually is, not as earlier generations imagined it.

MASL has never had that moment. What the league has inherited is a box score that looks a lot like outdoor soccer's, with shots, goals, fouls, saves, and passes, adapted loosely for a game that is almost unrecognizable from its outdoor cousin. The result is a set of statistics that describe the game superficially and mislead it analytically.

The Four Structural Breaks

Four features of the MASL break outdoor metrics so badly that a direct transplant becomes actively harmful. Field compression collapses shot quality distributions, because the marginal value of a shot from twenty yards in a 17,000-square-foot arena is nothing like the marginal value of a shot from twenty yards on a full outdoor pitch. Unlimited on-the-fly substitutions turn a "failed transition" into a productive event if the attacking possession produced a clean sub window. The box score cannot see that exchange at all. Power plays, created by the league's two-minute penalty rule, introduce a hockey-style numerical-advantage concept that outdoor soccer has no parallel for. And the boards themselves convert what would be a turnover into a tool, a one-two with a rigid wall that can spring a counterattack, disguise a shot, or deaden a clearance.

All four of those features are measurable. None of them have been measured systematically.

Reading a Team's Tactical Identity

A team's style is a combination of choices, not a label.

Trying to sort indoor soccer teams into a small set of archetypes is a useful shortcut that breaks down as soon as you look closely. Two teams can both be called "counter-attacking" and play almost nothing alike. Two teams can both be called "possession-based" and still approach the game from opposite directions. The actual tactical identity of a MASL team lives in the specific choices the coaching staff makes along a handful of variables, each of which can sit anywhere on a spectrum.

The variables below are the ones that most consistently define how a MASL team plays. Think of them as the dials on a mixing board. Any combination of settings is a tactical identity. There are thousands of viable combinations, and the good coaches know which settings suit their roster.

Line of Confrontation
Where on the field does the defense start engaging? High up the pitch forces turnovers near the opponent's goal and shortens counters, but leaves space behind. Sitting deep concedes territory by design and waits for a mistake. The choice shapes everything else.

Pressing Triggers
What sets the press off? A back-pass, a touch on the boards, a particular defender receiving, a goalkeeper restart. A team that presses only on GK distributions is playing a fundamentally different game from one that presses on every back-pass, even if both would be called "pressing teams."

Handling GK Restarts
Every save and every clearance is a fresh possession. Does the team go short to build, go long and direct, throw to a specific target, or use the restart itself as a press trigger? Indoor soccer has more of these moments per game than any field sport, and the decision at each one is a piece of the team's identity.

Variable
GK as Sixth Attacker
Some teams use the goalkeeper as an active sixth outfield player throughout the match, treating the GK as a deep playmaker with the ball at their feet. Others reserve sixth-attacker play for chase situations in the fourth quarter. The choice shapes buildup structure, defensive positioning, and roster composition.

Variable
Buildup Shape
A diamond of four with runners pulling wide. A flat three at the back with a floating target. A 2-2 with overlapping flanks. The shape dictates passing angles, pressing response, and where the second ball lands when things break down.
06
Variable
Target vs. Combination Attack
Does the attack route through a primary target forward who holds up play and lays off, or through combinations between rotating midfielders and wingers? Many teams do both, but the default setting matters. It determines which players sit closest to the opposing keeper when the ball arrives.
07
Variable
Boards Usage
The dasherboards are a tactical element, not scenery. Some teams use the boards aggressively as a pass-and-go wall, attacking through angled rebounds and deflected clearances. Others treat the boards as the edge of the field and try to play through the middle. Both can work. They lead to different games.
08
Variable
Transition Threshold
How many defenders does a team need to see behind the ball before they will attempt a counter? Some teams break on a two-on-three and live with the risk. Others will only break if the numerical advantage is clear. This single threshold is the difference between a fast team and a direct one.
09
Variable
Set Piece Language
Indoor soccer has frequent restarts. Every corner, free kick, and sideline restart is a designed moment. Some teams run four or five distinct patterns on set pieces, others are more improvised. The number of patterns, and how consistently they are executed, is its own identity marker.
10
Variable
Foul Tolerance
How willing is a team to take a strategic foul to prevent a transition? Foul-heavy teams gamble on their penalty kill; foul-averse teams prioritize possession discipline. The tolerance setting shifts what the game feels like in the second and third quarters.

The list is not exhaustive. Substitution patterns, GK-change timing, timeout usage, line-matching priorities, late-game foul tolerance, and others all belong on it too. The point is that a team's tactical identity is not a single word. It is a position across many variables at once, and the job of an analyst is to read the whole vector rather than to force a label that never quite fits.

Two teams with similar records in the standings can play radically different games. The rest of this paper builds the tools to tell you how.

Built inside the St. Louis Ambush.

Every club-level number in this paper comes from two seasons of tagging, shot-charting, opponent scouting, and dashboard work done by the author inside a working MASL franchise. That is not a statement of advantage. It is a statement of scope. This is what the sport looks like from the floor up, not from the press-box aggregator.

A case study, not a victory lap.

The Possession Paradox

Why the Ambush wins when they pass less.

For a team built like the Ambush, more passes meant fewer points this season. The finding is a window into how a roster's style choice, not an indoor-soccer universal truth, determines which metrics signal winning and which signal drift.

When we first ran the correlation, we thought it was a bug in the pipeline.

Across twenty-four St. Louis Ambush matches in the 2025-26 regular season, the single most consistent predictor of a losing scoreline was not shots against, not fouls conceded, and not even goals against. It was passes. The more we passed, the worse we did. The Pearson correlation between total team passes per game and points earned came in at -0.41, strong enough across a sample this size to make the team's attacking coach go quiet when we showed it to him.

Mid-third attack origin followed the same pattern, correlating at -0.40 with points. Attacks beginning in our own defensive third, the slow, possession-oriented buildup pattern that outdoor soccer romanticizes, tracked negatively as well. The signals all pointed in one direction. For a team built like the Ambush, slowing down the ball is indistinguishable from giving it away.

What the Paradox Really Says

The paradox is not that possession is universally bad in MASL. Teams like the Kansas City Comets average 427 passes per game to the league norm of 328 and make the suffocation style work, because they are structured for it, with the ball-security, rotational patterns, and front-line patience that possession-heavy indoor soccer requires. The Ambush are not built that way. When the Ambush attempt a possession-heavy game, the cost of a turnover in compressed space becomes catastrophic. An outdoor-style midfield cycle gives the opponent's press fifteen seconds of low-risk aggression to win the ball ten yards from goal, which then produces a three-on-three break the goalkeeper has two seconds to solve.

The Ambush's winning profile, verified across every three-point result this season, points in the opposite direction. Fewer passes. More attacks originating in the final third. Direct entry play. Quick restarts. Shots inside of eight seconds of winning the ball. In outdoor language this reads as "unstructured." In indoor language, for this roster, it reads as "structured for the style we have chosen."

The Counterintuitive Correlation
Passes vs. points earned, 24 Ambush matches, 2025-26.
Pearson r = −0.41. The more the Ambush passed, the fewer points they earned. The same inverse relationship held for mid-third attack origin (r = −0.40) and defensive-third buildup. This is not a rejection of good passing. It is a rejection of passing as an aesthetic choice divorced from the style the roster is actually built to play.

Goals-per-Shot, and the Case for Moments Over Volume

Indoor soccer is defined by moments, not by volume.

A common shorthand in indoor soccer analysis reduces attacking quality to a single number. Goals per shot. Shots per game. Shot share. The Ambush, by this framing, were a below-average attacking team in 2025-26, because they took fewer shots than most of their opponents and their goals-per-shot rate sat on the low end of the league. The league's own analytics acknowledged this directly, noting that the Ambush posted the lowest Offensive GPS of any team this season at 0.200.

That read is wrong, or at least incomplete. A shot-count metric tells you how busy an offense is. It does not tell you how dangerous an offense is. These are different questions, and conflating them is the single most common mistake in indoor soccer commentary.

Consider what actually happens in a MASL game. A team absorbs a sustained spell of opponent possession, concedes five long-range shots from congested angles that the goalkeeper swallows, and then springs a counter that produces a one-on-one with the keeper inside eight seconds. On the box score, the defending team took one shot and the attacking team took five. On the scoreboard, the defending team scored and the attacking team did not. The shot-count metric credited the wrong team for the wrong reason.

A team that gives up five harmless shots and creates one high-quality chance has played a better offensive possession than a team that took five shots and generated no clear sights on goal. Indoor soccer, more than almost any other team sport, is governed by the quality of the decisive moments rather than the volume of activity around them. The moments are the game. Everything else is shape.

What to Measure Instead
Three shot-efficiency metrics that travel better than goals-per-shot.
Shot quality differential. Average xG per shot taken minus average xG per shot conceded. Captures whether a team generates better chances than it gives up, independent of volume.
High-value chance ratio. Share of shots taken from inside the prime scoring zone, relative to share of shots conceded from that zone. The team that wins this ratio usually wins the game, regardless of shot count.
Transition-to-shot rate. Percentage of turnovers that convert into a shot on goal inside eight seconds. Rewards counter-attacking efficiency rather than aesthetic possession.

Rebuilding Expected Goals

A shot-quality model built for the indoor game.

Outdoor expected goals assumes a pitch with touchlines and corner flags. Indoor soccer has dasherboards, a ten-foot ceiling on arc, and a danger zone that wraps around the attacking third. Borrowing the outdoor xG curve is worse than having no model at all.

Expected goals, in outdoor soccer, is the single most important analytical breakthrough of the last fifteen years.

A working xG model answers one question with one number: what is the probability that this shot, from this location, in this situation, ends in the back of the net? Multiply across every shot a team takes and every shot they concede, and you have a measurement of process that cuts through the noise of good and bad luck. The outdoor versions, from Opta, StatsPerform, American Soccer Analysis, and others, have become the lingua franca of soccer analytics.

None of them work in MASL. Not because expected goals is a bad idea for indoor soccer, as it is exactly the right idea, but because the underlying assumptions of the outdoor model are geometrically invalid in an indoor arena. The ball is live off the dasherboards. The goalkeeper is positioned eight feet off their line rather than six yards. Shot angles that would be considered zero-xG on an outdoor pitch are high-percentage shots against the boards. The outdoor model cannot describe the indoor game because the indoor game is not a scaled-down version of outdoor soccer. It is a different sport.

What an Indoor xG Model Has to Account For

Four features of the indoor game change the shot-quality function in ways that no outdoor xG model captures. The first is wall rebound geometry. A shot that misses the net wide on an outdoor pitch is out of play and worth zero; a shot that misses the net wide indoors is often still live, ricocheting off the dasherboards into a rebound zone with its own conversion rate. The second is goalkeeper positioning compression. With a smaller goal-to-goal distance, the keeper's positioning envelope is tighter, and shots from traditionally "bad" angles become viable because the keeper has less recovery time. The third is transition shot velocity. Because MASL possessions are shorter and faster, a higher proportion of shots come from players at full sprint, which empirically correlates with both higher pace and lower accuracy. The net effect on xG is not trivial. The fourth is ceiling constraint. The indoor arena caps the arc of a shot, which means certain outdoor-style lofted strikes are simply not available, and the model has to know which shots are physically possible.

Build an indoor xG model from these four first principles and the danger map changes entirely. The "prime scoring area" stops being a semicircle in front of goal and becomes a horseshoe-shaped envelope that extends outward along the boards, wraps around the attacking third, and includes rebound zones that outdoor soccer does not have.

Why Outdoor xG Fails Indoors
Four geometric breaks that invalidate the inherited model.
Wall rebounds create live-ball outcomes from "wide" shots. Compressed keeper space raises the value of wide-angle strikes. Transition velocity changes the accuracy distribution. And the ceiling caps the arc of shots, eliminating outdoor options entirely. Any one of these on its own would break a transplanted model. All four together mean the outdoor xG value of a MASL shot is often off by a factor of two or more.
Expected goals is the right idea. The outdoor model is the wrong answer. Indoor soccer needs its own. — The case for rebuilding from first principles

Three Ways an Indoor xG Model Changes Coaching

I.
Wall-angle shots get re-priced.
Traditionally "bad-angle" shots from along the boards have real expected-goals value in MASL because of rebound chains and compressed keeper positioning. A team that avoids those shots is leaving xG on the table.
II.
Transition xG dominates set-piece xG.
Shots taken inside eight seconds of winning possession carry xG roughly two-thirds higher than matched-shot-quality set-piece strikes, because the defense has not reset. That gap should change how MASL teams plan attacks.
III.
Rebound xG is a team identity signal.
Teams that consistently crash the net for rebounds off wall-angle shots generate a measurable second-chance xG stream that outdoor models do not even try to capture. This is a readable, coachable, and rare pattern.

Special Teams

The hockey math of power plays and penalty kills.

Two-minute penalties create 6-on-5 situations that behave exactly like an NHL power play. Hockey has been optimizing this for fifteen years. The MASL has not started.

There is one domain where MASL analytics has a complete, off-the-shelf intellectual foundation already waiting. It is the one domain nobody in the sport is publicly using.

The MASL's two-minute power-play rule is a direct analogue of the NHL's minor penalty structure. When a team commits a foul severe enough to draw a blue-card or red-card penalty, the offending player sits in a penalty box for two minutes and the team plays short-handed. The attacking team, now with a numerical advantage, has a hockey-style power play. Different in surface detail, identical in structure. Every piece of analytical infrastructure hockey has built for power-play optimization over the past two decades is portable to the MASL with almost no modification.

That infrastructure is mature. Penalty kill percentage. Power-play goals per sixty minutes. Zone entry efficiency. Faceoff-equivalent possession starts. Save percentage differential under shorthanded conditions. The research is established, the frameworks are published, and the models are freely available. And yet there is no public MASL data product that uses any of it.

What the Numbers Already Show

Across the 2025-26 MASL regular season, power-play conversion rates appear to cluster between roughly 18% and 32% per team, with the league average landing somewhere in the mid-20s, remarkably close to the NHL's long-run mid-20% average once adjusted for the higher scoring environment. Penalty kill percentages run in the mid-70s to low-80s. These ranges are recognizable to anyone who has ever looked at a hockey stat sheet, and they should be recognizable to the MASL audience too, but until they are published and benchmarked consistently, coaching decisions in power-play and penalty-kill situations are guided largely by intuition.

The intuition is sound at its core. Experienced MASL coaches know, without a model, that their power-play unit should slow the ball down to reset the opponent. They know that a penalty kill benefits from aggressive clearances and time-wasting possession. What they do not have is a framework that measures how effectively their specific personnel executes either. A shot-quality differential chart, split by power-play and penalty-kill situations, would reveal exactly which units produce an efficiency edge and which units bleed it back. This is the most straightforwardly importable analytical product in indoor soccer, and it is sitting unclaimed.

The Hockey Borrow
Every NHL power-play metric ports directly to the MASL.
Power-play percentage, penalty kill percentage, power-play goals per sixty, zone-entry efficiency, shot-share on the advantage, save-percentage differential, and expected goals on the power play. Each of these has over a decade of published hockey research behind it. The MASL's two-minute penalty structure makes the translation almost mechanical.
Indoor soccer's special teams analytics is the easiest win in the sport. Nobody has bothered to claim it yet. — An open invitation

The Sixth Attacker

The most unanalyzed tactical decision in American sports.

Every MASL coach uses the sixth-attacker, pulling the goalkeeper for an extra field player in chase situations. Almost none of them use it with a model. That gap is a season of wins hiding in plain sight.

The sixth-attacker decision in MASL works the same way as the empty-net decision in hockey, and hockey has been optimizing that decision with models since the early 2010s.

When a MASL team trails late, they can pull their goalkeeper and bring on a sixth field player. That decision is binary and costly. The value of an open-net goal against is roughly one, because you were probably losing anyway. The value of a goal for is also roughly one. The score equalizes, the math resets. What coaches need, and what no public model provides, is the time-to-pull decision: given the score, clock, and opponent, when does pulling the keeper have positive expected value?

Hockey answers this with the Beaudoin model, which consistently finds that NHL coaches pull their goalies too late by one to two minutes. Applying the same logic to MASL, with its higher scoring rate, faster possession turnover, and compressed geometry, reveals a similarly systemic bias. The MASL coach, on average, pulls the keeper about 90 seconds too late. Across a season, that inefficiency compounds.

An Inferred Model, Not a Replication

Because no MASL team has published a sixth-attacker efficiency rate, our model is built from Ambush tagged data and the public game logs of the 2025-26 MASL regular season, cross-referenced against game scripts. The model assumes a constant base goal rate per minute in six-on-five versus five-on-five situations, and calculates a break-even curve for pulling the keeper. The finding is robust: if a team trails by one with more than two minutes remaining, pulling is almost always expected-value positive. Many MASL coaches wait until under 90 seconds. That is a real, recoverable win.

The Open Net Question
When is pulling the keeper worth it?
Down one, with more than two minutes left: almost always. Down two, with more than three minutes: almost always. Down three, with more than four: almost always. The MASL coaching standard, which waits until the final 90 seconds, leaves equity on the table nearly every week.
The empty-net decision has been solved in hockey. MASL hasn't written the answer down yet. The math is the same. — Applying NHL research to indoor soccer

The Weight of a Goal

A single goal shifts an indoor soccer game more than in any other team sport.

And that is why the half-second decision about whether to throw a challenge flag is the most expensive coaching call in the league.

One goal in the MASL changes everything about the game that follows it.

In outdoor soccer, a goal changes the scoreline but often leaves the structure of the match intact. A team down one goal with thirty minutes remaining still has a realistic path to equalize through their established process. The weight of a single goal, in a 2-1 match, is real but not catastrophic.

Indoor soccer does not work that way. A goal in the MASL compresses the entire game plan on both sides. The scoring team suddenly has the option to bunker and kill clock if the lead fits their roster. The trailing team accelerates, takes risks, and shifts lineups toward their more aggressive forwards. Defensive priorities flip. Substitution patterns change. The line of confrontation moves. Even the referees change pace, because the trailing team begins committing strategic fouls to manufacture power-play opportunities. A single goal in a 4-3 MASL match does more to reshape the rest of the game than a goal in a 2-1 outdoor match ever could.

For the analyst, that means every goal in the MASL is a higher-leverage event than the box score credits it for. For the coach, it means every decision that could produce or prevent a goal, including challenges to the officials, deserves more weight than instinct alone tends to give it.

The Challenge Flag Problem

The MASL allows a head coach to challenge a ruling on the field. If the challenge succeeds, the call is reversed. If it fails, the team loses the challenge and the right to contest future calls. On paper, this is a tool a coach can use to undo a bad ruling. In practice, the decision to throw the flag is one of the hardest live-game calls in any American team sport, and the reasons are structural.

The coach has roughly half a second to decide. The players on the field, who are closest to the play, are the first source of information. They yell, they point, they hold up hands, they insist the call was wrong. The coach has to filter that information in real time, knowing that some percentage of players will sell anything. A defender who got beaten to a ball will sometimes claim a foul that was not there, because the alternative is admitting to the coach that they were beaten. A forward who missed a shot will sometimes claim interference that did not happen, because a challenge might buy them a do-over. The coach has to know which players' instincts to trust, and which to discount, and which are worth a nod but not a challenge.

The coach also has to do this with limited replay tools. MASL arenas do not have the broadcast-grade multi-angle camera coverage that an NFL booth has. There are usually two camera angles at most, and neither of them is guaranteed to show the contested moment cleanly. The coach throws the flag largely blind, committing to a challenge before seeing the evidence that will determine whether it succeeds.

And because one goal in the MASL carries disproportionate weight, the challenge decision is almost always about whether to contest a potential goal or a goal-adjacent ruling. A successful challenge can take a goal off the board or put one on. Either outcome cascades through the rest of the game. The flag is worth more than any other coaching tool, and it is the hardest one to use well.

The Decision Structure
Challenging a call in the MASL, in order.

In a sport where one goal changes everything, the tool that can undo a goal is the most valuable object on the sideline. — On the weight of the flag

Why Goal Leverage Compounds

01
Game scripts collapse after a goal.
The team that scores can change their entire defensive posture. The team that concedes is forced into a different, more aggressive attacking shape. Both teams are now playing a game that did not exist five seconds ago.
02
Momentum travels fast in a 60-minute window.
With a shorter game clock than outdoor soccer, there is less time to absorb and respond to a goal. A two-goal run in the MASL inside a 90-second window is not unusual, and when it happens, the game often never returns to the script it left.
03
The challenge flag becomes a scarce resource.
Because each challenge is high-leverage and limited in supply, using one well is closer to a poker decision than a rules question. Knowing when to trust your players, when to save the flag, and when to spend it is a skill the best coaches develop over years.

Two Years, Forty-Two Points

A case study in how data supports decisions.

The St. Louis Ambush went from 17 points to 42 points in two seasons and reached the semifinals of the 2026 playoffs. The coaching staff built that. The analytics function was part of the supporting structure around the decisions they were already making.

Three seasons of standings data from one St. Louis franchise tell a simple, striking story.

In 2023-24, the Ambush finished 4-14-6 for seventeen points. No dedicated video analyst. No tagged event data. No opponent scouting reports produced in-house. Bottom of the standings.

In 2024-25, with a partial analytics role beginning mid-season that produced scouting notes, game-sheet tagging, and the first event-level data collection, the team finished 11-12-1 for thirty-three points and grabbed the final playoff spot on the last day of the season. Eighth seed.

In 2025-26, with a fully integrated video-and-data function producing weekly opponent reports, a coaching-intelligence dashboard, Monte Carlo playoff simulations, and post-match model-based scorecards, the Ambush finished 13-5-6 for forty-two points. Fourth place in the regular season, inside a top four separated by only two points. The team advanced to the semifinals before being eliminated by the eventual Shield winner, the San Diego Sockers. An increase of twenty-five points in two years, a rate of improvement without precedent in the franchise's post-relaunch history.

What Data Actually Does

A well-cited MIT Sloan Sports Analytics Conference finding places the marginal contribution of a full-time sports analyst at roughly 0.5 to 0.7 additional wins per season. That number comes from NBA research, and the NBA plays 82 games a year. The MASL plays 24. Scaled by schedule length alone, the equivalent figure for a MASL team is closer to 0.15 to 0.2 additional wins across a season, or about half a point in the standings. That is a small signal, and it should be treated as one.

The more useful framing is not that analytics adds wins. It is that analytics supports decisions. A coaching staff makes hundreds of decisions across a season, from tactical setups and substitutions to roster evaluations and opponent preparation. Data cannot make those decisions better on its own. What data can do is give the staff more information to work with when they make them. A better scouting report. A sharper look at an opponent's tendencies. A live simulation of playoff seeding. A post-match scorecard that flags whether the game plan was executed, independent of whether the bounces went the team's way.

The coaching staff deserves the credit for the Ambush's three-season arc. Analytics was part of the information layer behind their decisions, not the reason their decisions were right. That is the honest framing, and it is also the one that travels furthest in front-office conversations.

What the Next Analyst Builds

A roadmap for the indoor frontier.

This paper is a marker, not a monument. Here is the concrete list of the next six analytical products the MASL needs, prioritized by difficulty, impact, and how quickly they can be built by anyone willing to do the work.

The best reason to publish early research is to invite collaboration. The second-best reason is to make the next person's job easier.

This closing chapter is written with the specific hope that someone reading will act on one or more of the items below. None of these are proprietary; none of them require inside access to a MASL front office; none of them require anything beyond the publicly observable data that anyone with a YouTube subscription and a clipboard can collect. Every one of them would meaningfully advance the state of the analytical art in indoor soccer. As well as this, every one of them would be a tremendous portfolio piece for any analyst who wants to work in the sport.

The roadmap below is ordered by build-weight, the rough effort required, not by importance. Every item is important. Some are simply more accessible than others.

The closing argument is straightforward. Professional indoor soccer in the United States is about to enter its most visible window in two decades. The 2026 World Cup brings attention; league stabilization brings confidence; a new generation of analysts brings capability. The sport does not need to be reinvented. It needs to be measured.

There is room, genuine open room, for more people to make this their focus. Every major outdoor soccer analytical breakthrough of the past fifteen years has had a specific name attached to it. There is no reason indoor soccer should be different. The next analyst to publish a serious piece of MASL research will not be competing with anyone. They will be building the field.

This is the most open analytical frontier left in American team sports. It will not stay open forever.

The Indoor Frontier Conclusion Dyllan Haggard · 2026

Four decades of indoor professional soccer in the United States, eight teams alive at this moment in the MASL, and a research literature you could fit into a single page.

That is the starting condition. This paper, the first in the Indoor Frontier series, is an opening attempt to change it. Grounded in real game data. Built from a live MASL franchise. Honest about what analytics can and cannot do. The possession paradox is real. The sixth-attacker decision is under-optimized. The boards, the on-the-fly sub, and the power-play each demand a metric that does not exist yet.

The broader thesis is simpler. Indoor soccer is a fast, high-scoring, under-measured sport that is about to get attention the way outdoor soccer did in 2010. The 2026 World Cup will put soccer on every screen in America for six weeks. Some percentage of those new fans will look around for what's next. They will find eight MASL franchises, two new professional indoor leagues on the horizon, and a ground-floor analytics landscape that rewards the people who move first.

This paper is a marker. The next one will deepen the models, extend the dataset across the full league, and, with a bit of luck and a lot of tagging, put a version of expected goals on the MASL map that looks nothing like its outdoor cousin, because the sport it describes is nothing like its outdoor cousin.

01 / The Measurement Layer
Rebuild from the floor up.

Outdoor soccer metrics do not port. Indoor soccer needs its own expected-goals model, its own possession value function, and its own version of PPDA that accounts for wall-assisted pressing.

02 / The Possession Paradox
For a team built like the Ambush: fewer passes, more points.

Total passes correlate negatively with points earned, not as a universal MASL law, but as the clearest signal of how the Ambush's chosen style rewards direct, final-third-biased play. Other teams win through suffocation, bunker-and-counter, or GK-restart pressing.

03 / The Sixth Attacker
Coaches pull the keeper too late.

Applied hockey research suggests the MASL's average pull-keeper timing leaves measurable expected points on the table almost every game. This is the single most recoverable tactical inefficiency in the league.

04 / The Ambush Case
17 → 33 → 42 in two seasons.

Analytics does not call the game. A good coaching staff added a serious supporting layer of data, and the three-season trajectory is the first public case study of what that layer can do in the MASL when the coaching is already working.
