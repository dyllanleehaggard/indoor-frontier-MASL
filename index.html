About the project

A working analytical foundation for a sport that has never had one.

Indoor soccer has spent four decades operating without a public analytical foundation. The major North American sports each have a deep public statistical lineage — baseball's sabermetric tradition reaches back to Bill James in the late 1970s; basketball, football, and hockey followed in the decades after. Indoor soccer has had none of that. The Major Arena Soccer League is the modern continuation of a lineage that goes back to the 1970s, and yet, until now, there has been no published Pythagorean expectation for the league, no peer-reviewed expected-goals work calibrated to indoor possession patterns, no roster-construction analytics that distinguish the regular-season optimization problem from the playoff one.

The Indoor Frontier is an attempt to fill that gap. It is structured as a multi-volume research series, each volume tackling a specific analytical question in detail, with the data sources, derivations, and code published transparently. Each paper builds on the one before it. The first lays the foundational framework. The second resolves a structural paradox the first volume identified. The third, currently in development, will turn the structural findings into operational guidance for front offices and coaching staffs.

The work is independent. It is not commissioned by any team or league office. It is published openly, in part because the gap that prompted it is too large to fill privately and in part because the analytical conversation around indoor soccer needs more public participants, not fewer.

The Series

Three volumes, one trajectory.

From a foundational framework, to a structural paradox, to operational implications. Each volume reads independently; together, they form an integrated analytical case for how indoor soccer should be measured and built around.
I
Published · 2025

A Foundational Framework

The opening volume of the series. Establishes why indoor soccer requires its own analytical vocabulary instead of borrowed outdoor or hockey metrics, and proposes the first version of an indoor-calibrated expected-goals model. Covers possession structure, shot quality in confined space, the role of walls and substitutions in shaping play, and the historical context for why indoor analytics has lagged behind every other major sport.

Eight chapters · Foundational xG framework · ~30 minute read

II
Published · 2026

The Playoff Paradox

A retrospective of the 2026 Ron Newman Cup bracket and the first published Pythagorean expectation for the MASL. Derives the league's exponent (N = 2.76) from 149 team-seasons, applies it to the 2026 bracket, and explains via 100,000 Monte Carlo simulations why the regular-season leader has won the championship only 30 percent of the time across the league's modern era. The result is structural rather than tactical: the format is working as designed.

Eight chapters · Pythagorean derivation · Bracket Monte Carlo · ~40 minute read
→
III
In Development · 2026

Roster Construction & The Series-Level Player

The next paper in the series. Takes the structural finding from Volume II — that short-series formats reward variance reduction over offensive ceiling — and tests it at the player level. Quantifies low-variance contribution profiles, defensive consistency, and the asymmetric value of roster depth using player-game data from the modern MASL era. The aim is to translate the analytical findings into operational guidance for front offices and coaching staffs.

Player-level series performance · Variance & depth modeling · Expected publication 2026

How the work is done.

Each volume follows the same publishing standard. The data sources are listed, the derivations are written out in plain language, the code is structured to be replicable on a standard laptop, and the limitations are flagged honestly. The aim is for any reader with a quantitative background to be able to reproduce the central findings of any volume from the appendix alone.

The series is published as a set of long-form web essays designed to read well in a browser. Each volume includes an abstract, eight chapters of analysis, a conclusion, and a methodology appendix. Where charts and tables aid comprehension, they are built into the volume directly using inline SVG and styled tables, not as external image files. The result is intended to read as professional research first, web design second.

Data

Public, traceable, verifiable

Primary sources are MASL official records and the kenn.com all-time standings reference. Cross-referenced against contemporaneous reporting and team-published data where applicable.

Methodology

Standard quantitative tools

SciPy, NumPy, and conventional statistical methods. Pythagorean expectation, log-5, Monte Carlo simulation. No black-box machine learning where a transparent statistical model will do.

Code

Replicable in under a minute

All Python scripts in each appendix are short enough to read end-to-end. The full Volume II pipeline runs in under thirty seconds on a modern laptop with SciPy installed.

Style

Professional, accessible, plain

The series is written for a reader who is fluent in sports but not necessarily in statistics. Technical terms are introduced in context, not in jargon. The math earns its keep on every page or it does not appear.

About the Author

Dyllan Haggard

Lead Data Analyst · Video & Data Analyst, St. Louis Ambush (MASL)

Dyllan is a Lead Data Analyst at Stifel Bank & Trust in St. Louis, where his work focuses on credit analytics, CECL/ACL forecasting, and the data infrastructure behind portfolio risk management. In parallel, he serves as the Video and Data Analyst for the St. Louis Ambush of the Major Arena Soccer League, where he built the team's analytics function from scratch — including a Streamlit coaching dashboard, a two-app React PWA platform with a Google Sheets backend, opponent scouting infrastructure, and live in-game event logging.

He holds an M.S. in Data Science (GPA 4.0) and a B.S. in Computer Science with an Applied Mathematics minor (GPA 3.79), both from Fontbonne University. He is a co-founder of Footy Collective, an independent soccer analytics research group whose recent published work includes "The MLS Growth Series: The World Cup Effect" and "The USL Factor."
